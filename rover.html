Mission

  • Find subterranean water through the study of the soil geo resistivity with the method Vertical Electrical Sounding.
  • Detect and characterize lava tubes (hollow structures suitable for human shelter) through the study of subterranean structure with the method Ground-Penetrating Radar.
  • To develop an open source framework of a modular rover which works as baseline for future projects.
  • To establish the antecedent of a fully autonomous moon rover capable of communicating with other rovers conforming a constellation.
  • To establish the antecedent of a low cost and open source rover capable of carrying out its mission after the moon night.
  • Generate a database of images of the moon terrain and other measurements for boost future implementations of Deep Learning algorithms.

Mechanical Configuration

Our first moon rover

The rover is compact and its surface is comparable to that of an A4 sheet. Inside you will find the battery, the electronics boards, the motors and the cameras necessary for the mission.
It features 6 sets of flight heritage engines plus planetary geearboxes that will drive the wheels specially designed to navigate unknown environments.

Power

Survive one full lunar eclipse!

Thanks to the advancement of materials engineering, today we choose to have highly efficient solar panel modules with negligible weight, which guarantee us all the necessary energy to move our mechatronic system. They are located in the side because our first mission will be in the poles were the solar incidence it is almost parallel. Another decisive factor in our design is the batteries, a fundamental element that determines the success of our mission. We opted for centralized energy storage. It provides us with regulated voltage outputs for our control and power requirements, as well as enough energy density to keep our CPU in a controlled atmosphere while it performs data logging during 12 hours of operation.

Navigation

How does our rover manage to move and locate itself at the same time without teleoperation?

Among the wide range of autonomous robot navigation algorithms, we are using a strategy based on Visual SLAM. Our system is composed of a main stereo camera with wich, we can detect relevant "features" of the environment and perform a highly accurate localization and mapping of the lunar landscape.
The correct use of SLAM together with planning algorithms will allow our Rover to make autonomous decisions and decide where it has to move, always making an inference of parameters such as obstacles, valleys and craters.

Communication

Wifi in space!

The rover has three communication systems. The first is RS-422. It is used to check the "health status" of the rover before the mission begins. The second is IEEE 802.11n (WiFi 4) which is used to communicate with the lander and with other rovers with high speed (zone A - 500 m) and download all the collected information (geological and images). The third is LoRa, which lets the rovers communicate with each other within a 32 km radius (zone B) and recive commands and also transfer the collected information until the one closest to the lander can download that information. That way, the analysis area can be expanded.

Electronics

How is our rover controlled?

The brain of the rover is a microcontroller of the SAMV71 family, it will control different subsystems. To bring out the processing required for Visual SLAM, we count with a SBC (Single Board Computer), where the SLAM navigation libraries will be running. To achieve an easy implementation of the rover we pretend to use COTS (Commercial Of The Shelf) products, and open source libraries with the objective of reducing development and evaluation times and costs. Avoiding the use of rad-tolerant and rad-hardened components whenever possible, except for those developing a critical mission. To achieve a good reliability the rover will have redundant components that start working when a component fails.
